It isn't too confusing if you just say, "The rate at which acceleration
changes is most important for comfort.  Here is a graph of that rate of
change.  Some people call this jerk, it is a good word choice because when
you feel a jerk that is acceleration changing quickly."  And also, everyone
likes to learn a new way to use a word.

If you were looking at WHEN the accelerations are greatest that is actually
when the jerk is at its lowest. And the maxima of jerk are where the
acceleration curve is steepest, this could very well be when acceleration is
zero since that is the condition when the direction of acceleration is
changing direction.

In a way, because it is incorrect to discuss comfort as a direct function
of acceleration it is even more confusing.  You may be correct that the
systems that have the highest accelerations also have the highest jerk and
the least comfort, but it is physically not guaranteed this is so.  I can
imagine qualities of leverage and damping that make high jerk in low
acceleration conditions.

I picked this up working on the design of a roller coaster.  The transitions
from one direction to another could be visually not notable, but the "fee"l
of the transition would be all about the jerk, not the acceleration.

> Differentiate the data to report the correct measurand for comfort, or its
> lack - "jerk" is the term of art for the time rate of change of
> acceleration.  Acceleration is not always an informative measurand for
> comfort.  Acceleration can be quite comfortable - lying in bed for instance
> has 1G; more than anything you have measured with your iPhoney or iTouchy.
>  But the jerk is very small in bed, as opposed to on the trike.
>
>
>
> Acceleration is a good measure of the instantaneous force on a vibrating
> component. (F=ma)  Acceleration might tell you the magnitude of stress and
> strain and allow you to estimate whether a part will break and help you to
> optimize it.  But it is only indirectly, and in certain circumstances, an
> analog for comfort.
