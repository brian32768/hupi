It was Sachs cycle hydraulics that I first used, which later became part of SRAM. They recommended using light mineral oil.

Later using Maguras, including 'Big' and the same advice was given. They also always seemed to recommend fitting new 'olives' each time a connection was remade - maybe a clue to preventing seal deterioration? Or just routine replacement or a part that takes damage.

I wouldn't have said this was an incompatibility with 'normal' brake fluid as an imported 'Hayes' disc brake on the rear of my tandem was the first cycle hydraulic brake I encountered that followed the automotive DOT route rather than skin-safe mineral oil.

The Hope discs that Fran has were common on trikes of the era - the ones with the brake handle with a screw-in adjustment cylinder on each handle. They seemed to have a disadvantage that they suffered from locking up due to overheating when left in somewhere hot. The adjustment range of the cylinder was insufficient to adjust the range back to usability necessitating dumping oil as Fran mentioned. When temperatures drop, or if too much fluid is dumped, this can leave the brakes with the levers moving to no effect. 

Hope do a nice little bleed kit that looks like it might be the part for in-the-field bleeding and that takes pressure from a car inner tube but I've not worked out if it is compatible with the necessary generation of Hope hardware yet.

Rob

>>>> If you were to put mineral oil in a car brake system there would be
>>>> no assurance that the components and seals would have the expected
>>>> performance or life expectancy.
>>>> 
>>>> A designer of a modern braking system would most likely (to avoid
>>> liability
>>>> issues) select materials to go with the glycol ether and
>>> polydimethylsiloxane
>>>> that are typically used.  These should be OK if mixed together.  If you
>>> are
>>>> still in the business of selling and servicing trikes, you might want to
>>>> consider using approved brake fluids just to CYA.
>>>> 
>>>> Mineral oil was common in decades past; it is no longer considered a
>>>> quality brake fluid.  There is also an additive package blended with
>>> DOT X
>>>> fluids that extend their performance and life.
>>>> 
>>>> Food grade mineral oil will have no additives to prolong life, and all
>>>> mineral oils are fragile in comparison to the common brake fluids when
>>>> exposed to air and heat.  They quickly form polymeric sludges when
>>> abused.
