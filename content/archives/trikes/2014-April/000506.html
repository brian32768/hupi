Sadly, Conrad Oho, the gearing wizard of Marin County who let  
mountain bikes go uphill as well as down, has left us.  However, I  
have usually found that a bit of judicious work with a file will  
usually get a useful increase in range, if necessary.

Speaking of clean and lubed  - a custom system with just a guide to  
the rear cluster could be made using light U - sections of bent  
Coroplast or other light sheet stock to drain oil back into a little  
bath at the lowest point.  It wouldn't be picking up bits of greenery  
on every trip off-road, either.

>> Mea Culpa.  Back in 1980, I'd have bet that there would be more slick
>> magazines full of trike components than model aircraft mags by now.
>>  Instead, we are still struggling along with bicycle parts, sometimes
>> putting the rear derailleur cage closer to the ground than the  
>> wheel rim.
>>
>> What I've had on the drawing board for far too long is very  
>> different, and
>> need not be expensive to make.  It takes into account the need to  
>> guide the
>> chain against side forces, and rationalizes the whole affair for a  
>> RWD
>> recumbent tadpole trike.  The chain tensioner has always been used to
>> select rear cogs because it fits well there on a bike, but a  
>> simple cage,
>> as on the front derailleur is more than adequate for the slack  
>> run.  The
>> chain tensioner can be relocated closer to the middle of the run, to
>> replace the idlers often used,  and the moving idler can be just  
>> guided in
>> a straight line by a couple of loose slots in the chain guard and  
>> a spring.
>>  That provides plenty of take-up to run big, efficient chainrings and
>> super-wide range gears for a streamliner.  Efficiency on the rear  
>> cogs
>> falls off badly under 15t, and big front rings reduce the chain  
>> force,
>> reducing wear and frame flex.  They even give more room for a  
>> Browning type
>> shifter on the front, to work even under power, like the rear.
