In my experience, this is a big disadvantage of recumbents. In addition 
to the higher natural frequency of the inverted pendulum, the recumbent 
makes it difficult for the rider to maneuver his body for control, as is 
done on an upright bike. The inability to stand on the pedals for hill 
climbing is also frustrating for me. (OK, maybe I don't have my 
"recumbent legs.") Putting this all together, I find hill-climbing on a 
recumbent relatively difficult, and sometimes dangerous due to poor 
steering control. I'm sure this is more of a problem for me on 
Pittsburgh hills, than to flat-landers.

A couple ideas on how to mitigate this problem:
* A "landing gear" that is deployed for static stability during 
low-speed riding. This would be a great help for starting on steep 
hills. This has some problems with side slopes, and sharing the load 
with the driving wheel. Also, it can be pretty bulky and heavy.
* An assist motor, so you can maintain reasonable speed on hills. This 
could also help with starts, as you could keep feet down for balance 
while starting.
* A gyro wheel to help stabilize the bike (e.g. 
http://www.thegyrobike.com/). This works largely by generating steering 
inputs to maintain stability. I have played with this concept on a 
recumbent, and it seems to help. It does add weight and complexity, and 
requires power to run the gyro.

> I assume you want to compare moments of inertia, not angular momenta.  You 
> are correct that the smaller the moment of inertia, about an axis that 
> connects the contact points, the faster the object "falls" under the torque 
> of gravity.
> Specific comparisons depend on the details of the bike and rider 
> combination, of course.  With the spongy rider constituting the majority of 
> the mass, rider position makes a big difference.  Recumbents give somewhat 
> less flexibility of position than uprights, so the common approximation of a 
> rigid body works a little better for them than for uprights.
> The Physics principle is that the moment of inertia you are looking for is 
> the sum (or integral) of an infinite number of products.  The things you are 
> adding up are each tiny bit of mass times the square of its distance from 
> the axis referenced above.  Since the moment of inertia depends on how far 
> the mass is from the axis _squared_, it is generally not sufficient to 
> locate only the center of mass.  But, that will give you a very rough basis 
> for comparison.  Even rougher, ignore the bike's mass and simply locate the 
> center of mass of the rider (usually around the navel).  The higher that is 
> above the ground, the larger the relevant moment of inertia.
