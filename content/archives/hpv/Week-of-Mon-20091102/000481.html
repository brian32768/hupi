A couple of our fastest local riders in all conditions ride a  
lowracer. John Foltz and Chris ?. They can still drop nearly all  
uprighters, especially of similar fitness, even on uphills of all but  
steepest/longest grades.

I do note that they seem SLIGHTLY slower on the ups, but that doesn't  
hold them back much -- they can still push open a gap if need be. I  
suppose at that point they might be exerting MORE than the  
uprighters. But maybe not.

Often the speeds are such that they're still getting good aero perks.

I used to ride a lowracer with them. I think that excellent uphill  
performance came to all of us only after *a COUPLE YEARS of hard  
work*. The uphills did seem to take a *much SHARPER effort* than with  
an upright. Punchier, more forceful. It was a distinctly different  
feel than upright climbing and took serious longterm adaptation.

However, our lowracers back then were fairly heavy. (I've gone to  
more group-riding dynamics -- rotating pacelines, etc. -- and sold my  
HPV. I couldn't maintain the fairing in my limited space/time -- it  
was getting scrunched in my small garage, etc. I resented the 30+ lb  
bike, too.) The other 2 guys now ride ultralight carbon and seem very  
pleased with the results in all conditions. They may no longer need  
so much of the "sharp" efforts to put the uphill hurt on uprights.

My main point was the 2-year sharp effort adaption to superior uphill  
skills. Any testing/comparison would need to include that for fairness.

Possibly this is an "overhead" requirement that should be noted as  
part of the "cost" of 'benting. Such a learning (adapting) curve  
might be rejected in many activities. Well, chess is known to take a  
few years -- I suppose lots of highly technical activities are known  
to take a long time. Motor-racing, for instance.

--JP
