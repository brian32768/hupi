Barclay Brown <barclay at barclaybrown.com> wrote:

> a. Recumbents have aerodynamic advantages on flats and downhills so  
> that
> people I can ride with easily on the flats will usually be able to  
> outclimb
> me, thus it seems like the recumbent is slower on hills. If I ride  
> with
> riders that are too slow for me on the flats, I outclimb them just  
> fine.
>
> b. It has to do with the angle of the hill. Going up a hill changes  
> the
> angle of the riders legs relative to "flat" ground. So it makes it  
> harder to
> pedal. This is not very logical and this pains me, being an  
> engineer by
> training and career! Picture an extreme case--a bike where your  
> back is
> horizontal and the pedals are directly above your hips. Seems like  
> that
> would be harder to ride, since the pushing is all straight UP! So  
> when the
> bike turns up hill there is a little of this effect. Or this is all  
> in my
> head :) Sometimes I think that maybe if my seat raised an amount  
> equal to
> the rise in the road, it would be easier to climb.

I have noticed both a. and b. I've done steep climbs on a wide range  
of recumbent geometries and the ones that seem easiest to pedal on  
steep climbs are those with relatively upright seats and low bottom  
brackets while the very laid back high bottom bracket bikes feel like  
I'm working harder for a given heart rate. This could be in part  
because the more horizontal the body is the lower the heart rate is  
due to the way blood flow works in relation to gravity. I have one  
laid back bike with the bottom bracket much higher than the seat  
where it feels like I'm working very hard to maintain 140 beats per  
minute while another with a more upright seat and low bottom bracket  
it is fairly easy to maintain 150 beats per minute. I'd like to get a  
watt meter so I can see how the power output compares for various  
positions for a given heart rate.
