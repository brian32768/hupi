Hi Paul et al,

Yes, that's the mystery indeed. From a theoretical physics point of view,
there should be no difference in climbing, since we can neglect air
resistance, and the weight difference in bikes is easily overcome by rider
weight differences or even a bottle or two of water! To really get good
data, we'd need to control for rider fitness and weight, and there just
isn't enough data. 

What puzzles me, is ME. That is, anecdotal and experiential "evidence" that
seems to contradict or at least mitigate the obvious physics. I used to
train as much as many ultra distance riders, was about equivalent in
size/weight, total rolling weight about the same, easy to stay with them on
flats with no wind, but I would immediately fall off the back when we hit
even a modest hill, and I'd have to work like a DOG to stay with them. 

I intend no offense here either, but many recumbent riders just don't train
very hard, are overweight, etc. so they don't really provide good data here.
It's easy to dismiss their slow climbing as just a lower fitness or training
level (and more power to them for getting out and riding no matter their
level of course!). I would ask people on the list: compared to your riding
buddies, who ride about the same amount as you, are about the same age and
general fitness level (height/weight proportion, etc.) how do you climb
compared to them? If recumbents were no different than uprights, there would
be no difference. And I would find myself in the middle of the packs I ride
with. But I don't. You may say, well, you're just the weakest rider in the
pack, and perhaps so, but this is 10 years of experience riding with racing
clubs and fast sport clubs, large century rides, etc. where I'm normally the
only recumbent rider on the ride out of dozens of others. 

To the scientists on the list, I would put the challenge: come up with a
theory to explain the anecdotal experiences people keep sharing. WHY DO
recumbent have the reputation for climbing slowly. Your theory doesn't
explain this. Note for example, that no one is claiming that steel bikes
climb slower than carbon--there's no anecdotal evidence for this, so no one
need put forward theories. But there is for the recumbent thing. 

There's a principle in the scientific method that I'm sure you're familiar
with... that you can't use the data from which a theory was derived, as
evidence for the theory's validity. So one can't claim that because they did
this or that calculation or lab test and from that they concluded that X is
so, that this proves that X is so. One would need to test the theory
independently with new data. Where is that data that shows that recumbents
climb as well as uprights, controlling for weight? 

Hopefully this isn't road that's been traveled before in this discussion!

I really would like to get to the bottom of this climbing difference. Claims
that there is no difference don't seem convincing to my experience and the
anecdotal evidence. 

It's a nice day here in coastal NC... I'm going to go ride!

Barclay changed the subject, but I'll bite on the old hill climbing debate.
I think his suggestions for making climbing easier on a recumbent are very 
good, generally more practical than Ben Brown's (no offense).  Weight is 
your enemy here, so anything that adds weight (landing gear, gyros,...) is a

step in the wrong direction.  Unless that direction is toward designing an 
automobile.

The "big debate" he refers to does have a clear answer, at least one I can 
defend with basic physics.  I think the question may be simply stated: does 
either platform, upright bike or recumbent bike, have an _intrinsic_ 
advantage when climbing hills.  If we control for all other variables, in 
particular total mass and rider's power output capability, then the answer 
is no.  The rate of climb is directly proportional to Power divided by Mass.

This is easier to see if you restrict the comparison to long, steady, slow 
climbs, so that transient effects of engaging other muscle groups or 
different starting speeds become negligible.  If we consider lower 
aerodynamic drag as intrinsic to the recumbent, then the recumbent has an 
advantage even in climbing, but that advantage becomes vanishingly small at 
low speeds.

It is generally agreed that a reasonably fit rider can use all of his 
cardio-pulmonary capacity with his legs, so that max sustainable power is 
not significantly affected by standing on the pedals or otherwise utilizing 
the different positions offered by an upright.  A well-designed recumbent 
does not restrict breathing or add more friction of any kind.  The 
biomechanical stuff can get complicated, but the human body is extremely 
adaptable.  So if we allow for sufficient training, I would contend that 
there is no significant difference in the efficiency of delivering the 
rider's maximum power to the pedals, that is intrinsic to the recumbent 
position vs. the upright.

One could argue that greater mass IS intrinsic to the recumbent compared to 
the upright.  If so, then the advantage goes to the upright in proportion to

the mass ratio and how steep the hill is.  It remains generally true that 
for the same cost/quality level, an upright bike will tend to weigh less 
than a recumbent.  I think this contributes to the common perception, along 
with a possible sampling bias in the masses and fitness levels of the 
respective groups of actual riders.

Barclay's comments in his point a. are right on, and also a factor in group 
rides on flat terrain when there is wind.  On a headwind leg, I'm cruising 
with the bike club guys, no worries.  Comes a long tailwind stretch, and I'm

dropped like a physics class that is no longer required.  Seems ironic, but 
it makes perfect sense when you think about it.

I don't think there is any relevant objective reality in Barclay's point b. 
(no offense)
