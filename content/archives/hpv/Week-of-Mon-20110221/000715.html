Actually the book does cover a surprising range, but not "all" as John says, in
particular not his system. Quite a few commecially available low-power,
low-energy systems are described, and these tend to be geared toward low-speed,
low-range use, not the high-speed, long-range, intermittent use system. One 
system described ist the 1.9 kg Gruber assist for uprights, hidden in the seat 
tube, rumoured to be used by cheating racing cyclists...

Tom Mallard wrote:
...
> While fractional hp's is all that's required, having more hp's for the fluid
> drive means it operates at a much better efficiency than full throttle so in
> this case having more is better, should actually help mileage, so, that's why
> I chose 3-hp's as a starting point.

3 hp (2.2 kW) is quite hefty. I guess you would need this for most fluid drives 
which are probably not very efficient. I think mechanical systems are more 
efficient. Matthias Wegman built bikes with the "Tetz-philosophy". He used the 
surface of the rear wheel as an extremely wide-range CVT, the motor with a 
friction drive being able tro travel from near the hub (high gear) to near the 
rim (low gear).
