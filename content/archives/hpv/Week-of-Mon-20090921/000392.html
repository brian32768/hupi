I havn't seen the HP article yet, but edited a similar one in 2003:

see page 21 HP54: http://www.ihpva.org/HParchive/PDF/hp54-2003.pdf

The point was that one pedaller could spend a minute or so cranking up 
the flywheel, which could then operate at five times the average 
pedalling power for 15 seconds, e.g. to operate machinery which a single 
person wouldn't be able to power otherwise. This would apply to certain 
electrical tools as well, but I can't see the benefit of a "large" 
flywheel when charging batteries at a constant rate. There is of course 
the benefit from a "small" flywheel, because pedalling at constant 
torque is more difficult than pedalling at constant speed - rather like 
cycling fast is "easier" than exerting the same power cycling steep 
uphill (apart from the cooling of course).

However this still leaves a discrepancy. Human Power eJournal was also 
offered this article, but we returned it, as the previous one had taken 
Dave Wilson and me years to edit. There was a great deal of information, 
but we couldn't understand much of it and left this out, so in the end 
only three pages were left. I can imagine that there might be similar 
issues in the present article.
