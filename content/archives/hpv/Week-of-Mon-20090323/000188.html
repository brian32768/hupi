Gaines, William schrieb:
>  
> For one of the early IHPVA Symposiums, Dr. Alan Abbot presented a design
> that he built that was a somewhat encentified version of what John Tetz
> did.  In the Abbot version he set it up so that the harder that you
> peddled the more power you got! 

Yes, in the Fourth International Human Powered Vehicle Sysposium Proceedings of 
1994, pages 17-20: "An experiment to encourage physical activity throught the 
use of human power amplification", right before John Tetz's article pp 21-26: 
"AHPV: assisted human powered vehicle, a description".

Alan was one of the first to suggest this (he used an sensor on an idler pushing 
into the pedal chain) and maintained that the amplification "reward" could be 
used in any sized vehicle, his object being excercise in a motor vehicle, not so 
much assisting pedal vehicles. This work was used in the Charger electric 
bicycle marketed from California.

In Switzerland Michael Kutter developed one of the first sensor-controlled 
electric bicycles around 1992, the Velocity. He used a totally different system, 
a pedal crank speed sensor rather than a force sensor, and a mechanical system 
consisting of a planetary differential with the rear chain-cog casette on one 
side and the motor on the other side.

I didn't know this, thanks, Bill. The Tour de Sol in Switzerland also featured 
one or two "pushing" trailers. A German whose name escapes me devised a "phantom 
trailer" where the hitch is a force sensor which adjusts the trailer's motor to 
simulate a "massless" trailer, i.e. it doesn't help, but also doesn't hinder. 
This is a concept well worth exploring.
