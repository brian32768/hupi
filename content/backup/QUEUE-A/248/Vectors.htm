CROSS WIND, FORCE and VELOCITY VECTORS

HPeJ REFERENCE (after Nigel Jones), AUG 02, 2004

 

Consider a model train boxcar placed on a section of rail track. The wheeled vehicle may move forward or backward in a straight line but not sideways or any other angle, when rolled upon the track.

An air fan could be set to one side and slightly forward of the section of track. When turned towards the boxcar the flow of air (wind) will provide a force that will push against the boxcar. Because the vehicle's wheels are constrained to a single pair of tracks,  the direction that the boxcar might move will not necessarily be the same direction or angle as the wind's force originates from. Thus the direction of the fan's force can be understood as being divided into two different directions; in line with the the track (f' or force prime) and at a right angle to the track (f" or force double prime) . Only force prime (f') could cause motion in the direction of travel which is determined by the tracks. 

How strong (magnitude) the fan's wind force is and how it is redistributed may be understood  by drawing a right angle triangle that depicts the original force and it's resulting two components.

If the relative angle (radians or degrees) to the track and the magnitude (newtons) of "Force wind" is known then the portion of that force directed against the side of the tracks (f") and the portion of the wind's force directed upon the vehicle in line with the tracks (f') may be solved for.  These are force vectors.  In other words, a vector describes three relative qualities "magnitude", "direction", and "angle". 

Thus far only the concepts of force and force vectors has been explored, we have not yet accounted for the boxcar actually moving. Velocity is a vector as well, which refers to a certain a speed  (magnitude) occurring in a particular direction.

Now turn to imagine a bicyclist traveling on a windless day.  As he or she pedals along through the otherwise still air an artificial wind blows past the the cyclist that is of the same speed as the bicycle's speed over the road. The direction of this artificial wind occurs is in the same direction as the direction of travel.  Thus the apparent wind velocity equals the bicyclist's velocity (same speed, same angle, opposite direction, in other words a headwind.)

Now add to the vision, if the natural wind were to blow towards the cyclist from the same direction as the direction of vehicle travel. In this situation we could simply add the bicycle's speed and the natural wind's speed together to determine the bicyclist's air velocity. For example if the cyclist where traveling towards the Southeast (135 degress azimuth) at 3 m/s and the natural wind where blowing from the Southeast (135 degrees azimuth) at 2 m/s the flow of air over the cyclist would be 5 m/s; as 3 m/s + 2 m/s = 5 m/s.  The resulting force vector due to air resistance acting in opposition to  the cyclist's forward motion would also occur at the same angle relative to the direction of travel. Referring back to the above triangle depicting force vectors, f double prime (the sideways force) would equal zero and f prime would have a value not equal to zero. 

If the natural wind were to blow from any angle other than a "pure" head or tailwind the force vector existing at a right angle to the direction of travel will be greater or less than 0.00 newtons. Like the boxcar on rails, bicycle tires roll freely in the line of travel and resist sideways motion. In extraordinary conditions such as slick ice and a strong cross wind, this force component could concievably contribute to the tires slipping sideways over the road surface.

"V" is the natural wind's speed. "alpha" is the angle that the wind blows from relative to the direction of the bicycle's travel. "B" is both the speed and the velocity of the bicycle. "AW" is the apparent wind speed. "beta" is the apparent direction of the wind when accounting for both the natural wind and the motion of the bicycle. "S" is the side component of the natural wind's speed (much like f" above) which acts at a right angle to the direction of travel. "F" is the portion of the natural wind's speed in conjunction with the rate of travel  over the road that acts in opposition to the forward motion of the bicyclist. 

Of these values only "B" (ground speed), "V" (wind speed) and "alpha" (wind relative angle to the direction of travel) need to be defined or measured by direct observation.

Two of the values might be better understood as being velocity components rather than observable phenomena.
