ACHIEVABLE DISTANCE AS DETERMINED BY VELOCITY CHOICE

John Snyder
working draft 1999-2003, 2005

 

Summary

Average velocity choice influences the maximum possible distance traveled by a bicyclist.

 

Introduction

[figure 1] Duration of sustainable power.

A human's sustained power output determines how long that effort can be maintained before the onset of muscular fatigue, also referred to as physiological fatigue. To quote a from a popular rock and roll song of the past, "it's more than a feeling."  Physiological fatigue describes muscle tissue which after prolonged or intense exercise is no longer able to contract. A cyclist who has pedaled with sufficient effort to experience complete physiological fatigue of the legs' muscles will be subjected to a temporary inability to walk or even stand.    

The following equation predicts the duration of effort (an amount of time) a healthy human will be able to sustain a given quantity of power up until the onset of physiological fatigue. [fig 1]

duration of effort (hours) =13.31* EXP [ ( -0.51 * (0.027 * watts - 1 )]

The equation is a curve fit to plotted data collected during a large scale NASA sponsored study, conducted in the early 1960s, of "healthy humans" pedaling stationary ergometers until achieving physiological fatigue. [refs 1 & 2] 

A healthy human in this context will be regarded as an adult male who follows a moderately active lifestyle, yet is not a trained athlete [ref 2].   Few individuals will exhibit this exact level of fitness.  And, because the above key equation was indirectly derived from a stationary cycling situation how well it might apply to cycling in road conditions is tenuous.  It will be understood that this paper only strives to provide a qualitative rather than a quantitative handling. Thus no attempt has been made to solve for an exact numeric solution.

Work represents a force moved through a distance. The rate of work being power.

Work = force x distance
(joules) = (Newtons) x (meters)
Power = force x distance / time
(watts) = (joules) / (seconds)

A rate multiplied by its duration represents a total.  For example, if a person walks at a constant rate of 5 km/h she will have traveled 10 km after 2 hours.  In the same manner when the rate of work performed (power) is multiplied by it's duration, a numerical value reflecting total work results. Here, total work a healthy human might perform will be known by;

Total work = Power x duration of effort
joules = watts * |3600 * {13.31 * EXP[(-0.51 * (0.027 * watts - 1)] } |

 

 

[figure 2] Work performed at a rate of 1 HuP

Figure 2 depicts work performed as related to different time spans.  Maximum work possible (the highest point of the curve) will occur when a person of average fitness outputs approximately 75 watts for 8 hours. Seventy-five watts equates to 1 HumanPower (1 HuP). Continuously generating greater or less than 1 HuP decreases the amount of work which may be achieved by a  healthy human before requiring rest.

That 1 HuP facilitates the most work to be produced, by the theoretical healthy human does not imply a maximum cycling distance would be achieved at that unique power level.

Power and speed
For every given bicycle and rider, as the constant speed value changes the power requirement also changes in a predictable fashion.  [figure 3]  

Power = Force * velocity

Each of the accompanying graphs (figures 3,4 and 5) were plotted using the following inputs: 33 year old, male of average health, 65.00 kilograms, 1.80 meters in height (B = 0.87 kcal/sec) riding a bicycle whose Efficiency = 98.50% / vehicle mass = 10 kg / no slope / no wind / constant velocity / air density =1.20 kg/m^3 / CdA=0.55 m^2 / Crr=0.0085 (These same vehicle drag parameters are applied to figures 4 and 5).

 

 

 

 

 

 

[figure 4] Achievable Distance

Achievable Range
A specific power value was used with  the first equation to define a possible duration of effort.  Duration of effort times the associated velocity suggests a maximum distance a cyclist might be able to travel non-stop.

(duration of effort) * (velocity) = achievable distance

Figure 4's peak points to a specific constant velocity which optimizes "an average Joe's" effort to cycle as far as he can.  In this example, bicycling faster or slower than 3.6 m/s diminishes the total achievable distance.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

[figure 5] Energy consumption. 

Energy consumption
The amount of energy as food needed  to maintain a constant cycling velocity may also be predicted [fig 5].

A simple method to determine an individual's dietary energy requirement while bicycling begins with the rider's resting metabolic rate to which is added the energy requirement of exercise. This additional energy is estimated by multiplying together power and an experimentally determined constant. [ref 3, Jones]

E = B + (C x P)
where:
E = Total energy requirement (kcal/sec)
P = Power at pedals (watts)
B = Base load (kcal/sec)
C = constant ( 3.74 )

Energy requirement / velocity = energy use per unit distance

(kcal/s) / (m/s) = kcal/m

The lowest part of the curve in figure 5 suggests a specific velocity that most effectively conserves metabolic resources, i.e., least amount of extra food needed to sustain body mass while exercising. As before, the example vehicle and rider when traveling faster or slower than ca 3.5 m/s increases energy consumption per distance. The velocity choice which optimizes range may well also be the most energy frugal, hardly a concern for people who ride bicycles in order to assist weight loss.  But,  least possible energy expended per distance covered can be a matter of great importance in times or places where food is scarce.

"Students at Dartmouth College [ed. in 1972] used an ergometer to find out what power output an ordinary untrained bicyclist could maintain over useful periods of time. They found that for prolonged periods about 0.05 hp (37.3 W) was maintained with pedaling rates of 20 - 60 rpm. It can be calculated that this power would give a road speed of about 8 mph (3.6 m/s). This speed is commonly achieved by an average "utility" bicyclist ..." [ref 2, Whitt and Wilson]

 

 

 

[figure 6] Achievable range is influenced by a vehicle's drag characteristics

Optimal Speed
The preceding observations suggest that a particularly effective cycling velocity may well be intuitively selected by some cyclists which matches effort (power) to maximized product (distance). This optimal speed will vary depending upon the performance characteristics of the bicycle, the cycling environment, and upon the physical ability of the individual cyclist.

 

 

Parameter Sets
Parameter sets are measurable quantities which help to describe the amount of power is required to operate any particular bicycle. Parameters included but are not limited to coefficients of aerodynamic and rolling resistance, vehicle mass, and drive train efficiency  Manipulating only one parameter such as Cd*A (figure 6 utilizes the f.p.s. system) will result in changes in the total force in opposition to forward motion and the constant speed needed to achieve maximum possible distance traveled. 

Take special notice of the next example. Bicycle B which exhibits the shortest predicted range does not possess the least favorable aerodynamic drag properties.

As a general rule of thumb: lowering the any of the individual values representing aerodynamic drag , rolling resistance, and mass, and the raising the value for drive train efficiency results in a potential increase of the constant speed associated with a distance the hypothetical average human may be able to cycle without resting. No one performance characteristic is more or less significant than another. The importance of low parameter values has been based on an atypical scenario, flat, windless conditions without acceleration. Cycling in dynamic conditions will dictate a relative and fluxuating hierarchy of parameters in accordance with the cyclist's travel goals and the riding environment.
